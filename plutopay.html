PlutoPay

Welcome to PlutoPay, a digital wallet app that allows users to accomplish financial transactions such as paying, requesting, transferring and sending money, depositing checks, shop and even splitting and paying bills. All transactions can be done remotely without ever having to physically visit a bank or store. All activities are just a tap away.

My Design Process

01 - Discover

1.1 The Problem Statement

What problem(s) do users encounter?

There are many digital wallet solutions in the market today. Users need a way to send/receive/pay money that is safe and secure from any account/cards linked, split checks/bills/payments and even shop while using the app because they want be in control of their financial transactions.

What are viable solutions?

Create an app:

  • where users can send/receive/pay/deposit money for their financial transactions
  • where users can split payments evenly or simply input their own value
  • where users can withdrawal/pay money from the account of their choice giving them more control over their transactions
  • that will offer integration so that the user will seamlessly be redirected to the online shop offering them the ability to pay with the app during the checkout process
  • that will be intuitive and focused on giving the user control over what bank accounts/credit cards are used for distribution giving our user that flexibility and control they desire
  • that is safe and secure

02 - Define

2.1 User Personas

After my research, I reviewed the sessions, organized the data for each participant and used my research findings in the discovery phase to create two personas to help me further understand the users. They are representations of my target audience. Their behaviors, goals, needs, motivations, and frustrations are derived from my research participants, also aligning with the product requirements.

Who are they?

Shea is a from our Generation X group. She grew up with desktops but has had to become pretty savvy now that she owns her own business. She needs a solution that has security to protect her information along with transactions from her customers and that has an easy interface so that she doesn’t have to spend too much time trying to figure it out and possibly jumping to another tool that may fit her needs. She’ll need to be able to reference back to onboarding tutorials or easily navigate to Help Articles when she’s stuck. She loves shopping and frequently orders inventory for her business, so she’ll need an easy way to checkout. She not only needs efficiency, but she needs a solution that will save her time in the long run.

Max is from our Millennial group. He works hard, but also likes to play hard. He’s a little nerdy and loves technology. Max travels a lot and often finds himself splitting bills, fees, travel cost, etc. with his friends and utilizes his phone for these types of transactions. The last thing he wants to do is carry cash on him. He wants to travel lighter and wants to avoid bringing too many credit cards with him as he likes to use the ones that he will get the maximum points from his purchases. Since he is always on the go, he needs a way to easily view his transactions and filter through them so that he can have peace of mind that there isn’t any unauthorized transactions. Also, he would very much benefit from a bio-metric face recognition software, along with two factor authentication. If there is a problem, Max needs to be able contact to customer service easily.

What are they meant to do?

These two personas will help establish empathy with our users and prioritize functionality based on our audience in combination with user journey maps or storyboards that will help compliment our research efforts and ultimately build a successful product for our users.

05 - Test

5.1 Usability Testing

The mid fidelity wireframes were turned into a clickable prototype in Figma. I conducted a usability test to test the features and functionality needed to complete 3 tasks on my app. The goal of this phase was to understand if the features and overall purpose of PlutoPay were intuitive by collecting valuable feedback from testers in my target audience and to identify if there were errors and room for improvement.

Affinity Map & Rainbow Spreadsheet

After my usability testing, I needed a way to interpret my data. I created an affinity map. This really helped me isolate the varied bits of information, extracting them from my interview and transferring it onto paper so that I could easily group them so that I could see the patterns or behavior across the participants.

I then took this information and entered the participant info into a Usability Test Results tab to help me further identity patterns and classify my errors by using an adapted version of Jakob Nielson’s four-step rating scale, then allowing me to think and document possible solutions to help influence future iterations of my prototype.

Summary

This project has come a long way, from sketches of lo-fidelity wireframes, to designing mid-fidelity wireframes with Adobe XD, and then switching to Figma to create my High fidelity mockups and prototype has been invaluable. I’ve learned so much throughout this process with the most important being able to learn the principles of User Experience, then applying them to a case study, and even being able to pick up some best practices such as:

  • For a mobile design, I had more than 6 icons on the bottom navigation originally and I learned that best practice is to have 5.
  • I learned that my icons and avatars were to large so I went back and updated them, icons should be about 24x24 px.
  • Using the color gray gives off the impression that a prototype is not complete so I went back and updated all mockups to a light blue.
  • I could have saved so much time by activating layer grids earlier on in my project!

Though some of these challenges I encountered may be small, but applying these best practices to this project made such a difference by making my design uniform and more appealing to the eye.

Being able to perform user research, from interviewing, soliciting feedback from my mentor, peers, and interviewees has given me the ability to step in the shoes of another person and see their perspectives through their lens. Receiving this feedback along the way has allowed me to question my own decisions objectively and to step outside of my box to make changes that would further improve it while still staying true to my end goal.

This project has allowed me to value the process of iteration and reiteration. Testing and retesting design decisions to see what works and what doesn’t has taught me the importance of refining the design, knowing that there is always improvements that can be made and learning that no design is ever perfect. By opening up to feedback whether good or not, really allowed me to consistently focus on enhancements that otherwise may have been overlooked, further improving my product.

With this being my first UX case study, trial and error is inevitable. Making mistakes along the way has been a learning curve, allowing me to grow and to apply these lessons learned and best practices going forward. I look forward to developing my skills further and I am excited for the next chapter or my career.
